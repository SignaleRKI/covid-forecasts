About

The German and Polish COVID-19 ForecastHub aims to bring together short-term forecasts of the further course of the pandemic in Germany and Poland. Our goal is to systematically compare different predictions, identify particularly reliable models and assess whether combinations of different forecasts can yield more reliable results.

The platform is run by members of the Chair of Statistics and Econometrics at Karlsruhe Institute of Technology and the Computational Statistics Group at Heidelberg Institute for Theoretical Studies, with support from the Signale Team at Robert Koch Institut. The project is developed in close exchange with the US COVID-19 ForecastHub run by the Reich Lab at the University of Massachusetts, Amherst.

This project is public and all forecasts can be downloaded from our github repository. More ample information on this project can be found in the associated Wiki.

The German and Polish COVID-19 Forecast Hub also forms the basis of a pre-registered forecast evaluation study on which we plan to report in 2021. The protocol is available here.

Forecast Targets

We currently focus on short-term forecasts of confirmed cases and deaths from COVID-19. Both targets are addressed both at an incident (i.e. weekly new cases and deaths) and a cumulative scale (total cases and deaths). New forecasts are issued every Monday/Tuesday and cover forecast horizons of one through four weeks into the future. A detailed description of forecast targets can be found here.

Interpretation of Forecasts / Limitations

Our project aims at both providing forecasts in real time and evaluating systematically how reliable these forecasts are. Please keep the following in mind when interpreting forecasts:

  • COVID-19 forecasts have proven challenging in the past. Especially case forecasts have not always been accurate (as deaths are a more lagged measure they are somewhat easier to predict).
  • We collect model outputs up to four weeks into the future, but recommend focusing on forecasts one and two weeks into the future.
  • Confirmed cases up to 1 to 2 weeks ahead and deaths up to 3 weeks ahead are assumed to be largely unaffected by future changes in control measures (see here for a similar reasoning by the UK Scientific Pandemic Influenza Group on Modelling). Beyond these time horizons, outputs from most models should be interpreted as "baseline projections", i.e. projections under the assumption of no change in interventions, behaviour and testing. They are no longer valid if these aspects change.
  • Forecasters are asked to quantify the uncertainty of their forecasts via prediction intervals. However, the coverage of prediction intervals has empirically been lower than the nominal levels (i.e. 95% prediction intervals contained the eventually observed value in less than 95% of the cases). In other words, uncertainty has been underestimated and forecasts were on average too confident.
  • We expect the course of the epidemic around Christmas to be difficult to predict, so forecasts should be interpreted with special care.

Forecast Evaluation

One goal of this platform is to compare the forecast performance of different models systematically. Results will be included on this website shortly. An outline of the methods used for evaluation can be found in this preprint.

Ensemble Building

Each week we combine the different forecasts submitted to the platform into so-called ensemble forecasts. Such combined forecasts are known to yield more reliable predictions in many domains and are becoming increasingly used in epidemiology. See here for a recent preprint on ensemble forecasts based on the US COVID-19 Forecast Hub. Technical details are provided in this wiki entry.

More specifically, we provide three different ensemble models that are computed as follows:

  • KITCOVIDhub-median_ensemble
    An average of forecasts submitted by different models, computed as the median (per quantile).
  • KITCOVIDhub-mean_ensemble
    An average of forecasts submitted by different models, computed as the mean (per quantile).
  • KITCOVIDhub-inverse_WIS_ensemble
    A weighted average of forecasts submitted by different models, with weights computed from the average performance over the last three weeks.

Baselines

As a reference in our forecast evaluation study the Forecast Hub Team generates forecasts from two simple reference models:

  • KIT-baseline
    A naive model that always predicts that the number of new cases/deaths will be the same as last week (in expectation).
  • KIT-time_series_baseline
    A statistical time series model (exponential smoothing with multiplicative error terms).
